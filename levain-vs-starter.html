Levain vs Starter

What they are, how they differ, and when to use each

If you have baked a few loaves and started reading recipes more carefully, you have almost certainly run into the word levain sitting where you expected to see starter — and wondered whether it matters. It does, a little. Here is what you need to know.

Your Starter — The Living Archive

A starter is a small piece of living dough — flour, water, wild yeast, and bacteria — that you tend and store in the refrigerator. You can think of it as a culture you keep alive, the way someone might keep a jar of kefir grains or a ginger bug. It is refreshed on a regular schedule: a portion is discarded, fresh flour and water are added, and the remaining culture has enough food to stay active and healthy until the next feeding.

Your starter is not used up in a single bake. You pull a portion of it out to work with, and the rest goes back into the fridge. Over time — months, years — it becomes more stable and more expressive. It is, in a real sense, yours. It carries the wild yeasts of your kitchen and your flour.

For recipes that don't need a great deal of rise — flatbreads, pizza, some crackers — you can use your starter directly, straight from its jar when it is active and bubbly. It will work. It will taste good. It is the simplest approach.

A Levain — The Supercharged Offshoot

A levain is a one-time preparation made from a small piece of your starter combined with fresh flour and water, left out at room temperature to ferment until fully active. It is not stored or maintained — it is made fresh for a specific bake, used entirely in the dough, and that's the end of it. Your starter, meanwhile, is refreshed and goes back into the fridge as usual.

Because the levain has been given fresh food and left to work at room temperature for several hours, it arrives at its peak in a more vigorous, more bubbly state than your refrigerated starter would. Think of it as a supercharged starter — more active, faster-acting, and better suited to lifting a dough that needs a proper rise. When you put a levain into bread dough, the bread rises more quickly and more reliably on baking day.

We prefer to use a levain for most breads — anything enriched, anything that needs to hold a real crumb structure. A wetter levain (equal parts starter, water, and flour) is what we reach for most often. Made the night before, it is ready by morning: domed, full of bubbles, and smelling yeasty and a little sour.

✦ A Simple Overnight Levain

This is the basic formula we use for most breads here. Make it the evening before you plan to bake.

  • 2 tbsp active sourdough starter (from your jar, after a recent feeding)
  • ½ cup + 1 tbsp warm water (about 125–150ml depending on your recipe)
  • ½ cup einkorn flour (about 60g)

Combine in a clean jar or bowl. Stir well, cover loosely, and leave at room temperature for 6–10 hours — overnight works perfectly. In the morning it should be visibly domed and full of bubbles throughout. If your kitchen is cool, it may take a little longer; if warm, check it after 5 hours. Use it at its peak, before it starts to deflate.

Can You Use Starter Instead of Levain?

Yes — and most of the time it will work well. If a recipe calls for levain and you forgot to make one, use an equal weight of active starter that has been recently fed and is at or near its peak. The result may rise a little more slowly and the flavour will be slightly different, but the bread will still be good.

The one situation where the swap matters more is a recipe that needs a strong, fast rise — enriched doughs with eggs and butter, or breads that you want ready within a tighter window. In those cases, the extra vigour of a fresh levain earns its overnight preparation time.

What Does "At Peak" Mean?

Whether you are using starter or levain, you want it at its peak — the moment when it has risen as far as it will go and the dome is just beginning to hold or very slightly flatten. At this point the wild yeast and bacteria are maximally active and your dough will ferment with the most energy. A starter or levain that is past peak (deflated, with a sharp alcoholic smell) will still work but will produce a slower and more unpredictable rise. One that hasn't quite reached peak yet will also work, but less vigorously.

With einkorn starters specifically, the rise is often less dramatic than with a standard wheat starter. Don't wait for it to double — watch for a dome, an increase in volume of around 50–75%, and a lively bubbly texture throughout. That's your moment.

✦ The Name Game

Levain is the French word for sourdough — pain au levain simply means sourdough bread. In different baking traditions you will encounter the same basic idea under many names: lievito madre in Italian, surdeg in Swedish, masa madre in Spanish, Sauerteig in German. Every bread culture that ever worked with wild fermentation developed its own vocabulary. What they were all describing is the same living thing: flour and water animated by the yeasts and bacteria of a particular place.
