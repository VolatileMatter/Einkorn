Chapter VIII

Working with Einkorn

Learn the character of the dough before you try to shape it

"You really just have to get in there and try. It will take time to develop. But you'll get that feel over time, and nothing replaces it."

Einkorn behaves differently from modern wheat at every step. Its gluten is weaker. It absorbs water more slowly. It becomes gummy if over-mixed, and it is stickier than any commercial flour you have worked with before. Understanding its character — and adjusting your technique accordingly — is the entire key to success. The mistakes that ruin most beginner einkorn loaves are not random: they are all variations on the same root error of treating einkorn like modern wheat.

Baker's Percentages

Baker's percentages express every ingredient as a percentage of the total flour weight. Flour is always 100%. Everything else is relative to flour. This makes recipes easy to scale and easy to understand.

The Autolyse — Never Skip This

The autolyse is the step where you combine flour and water before adding your starter and salt, then leave the mixture to rest for 30–60 minutes. During this rest, the proteins in the flour begin to bond and form gluten naturally, without any kneading. The result is a dough that starts stronger, handles better, and needs fewer folds.

For einkorn specifically, the autolyse is not optional. Its weaker gluten needs every head-start it can get. Combine flour and water, stir until no dry patches remain, cover, and leave. When you return, the dough will feel noticeably smoother. Then add your starter and salt.

Hydration — Start Lower Than You Think

High-hydration doughs (80%+) produce the open, airy crumb you see in professional sourdough photos. They are also wet, difficult to handle, and unforgiving for anyone still learning. Start at 70–75% hydration. Your bread will be slightly less open, but dramatically easier to manage. As your instincts develop — as you learn what einkorn dough feels like at different stages — you can slowly increase hydration.

Stretch and Fold — Not Kneading

Traditional vigorous kneading is not appropriate for einkorn. Its weaker gluten breaks down under sustained mechanical force. Instead, use stretch-and-fold during bulk fermentation:

  1. Wet your hands thoroughly. Water prevents sticking better than flour, and avoids drying the dough.
  2. Grab one edge of the dough, stretch it firmly upward until you feel resistance, fold it over the centre.
  3. Rotate the bowl 90°. Repeat for all four sides. Flip the dough, tuck into a rough ball. Cover. Rest 20–30 min.
  4. Repeat 3–4 times total over the first 2 hours of bulk fermentation.

After each round, the dough should feel more cohesive. By the third or fourth fold, it should hold its shape when you pull an edge, and the surface should look smoother and show small bubbles. This is the gluten network developing.

The Poke Test

The poke test tells you whether your dough is ready to bake. Gently press your finger about ½ inch into the dough and watch what happens:

  • Springs back immediately: Under-proofed. Give it more time.
  • Springs back slowly, leaves a slight dent: Perfect. Bake now.
  • Doesn't spring back at all: Over-proofed. Bake immediately — you will lose some rise, but it is salvageable.
⚠ The Most Common Einkorn Mistakes
  • Over-mixing: Einkorn worked too hard becomes gummy. Stop as soon as ingredients are incorporated.
  • Adding flour to reduce stickiness: Wet your hands instead. Extra flour makes the final loaf dry.
  • Skipping rest periods: Einkorn needs time between folds to relax and hydrate.
  • Under-active starter: The single most common cause of failure. If your starter isn't doubling reliably in 4–8 hours, do not bake with it yet.
  • Under-baking: Judge by colour, not time. A pale crust has underdeveloped flavour. Go for deeply golden.
  • Slicing warm bread: Warm einkorn cuts gummy. Wait a full hour minimum.

The Curing Step

After baking, turn off the oven and return the bread (without the Dutch oven lid) with the oven door slightly ajar. Leave for 20 minutes. This curing step — learned in professional bakeries — allows the bread to dry out gradually and produces an exceptionally crackling, long-lasting crust. It is worth the extra wait.
