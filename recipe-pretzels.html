Sourdough Einkorn Pretzels
Deep brown, chewy, authentic — the baked baking soda trick changes everything

What gives a pretzel its deep mahogany colour, its distinctive chew, its characteristic bite? Not the dough — the bath. Commercial pretzels are dipped in a lye solution before baking, a caustic alkali that triggers the Maillard reaction and creates that shatteringly dark, crackly exterior. This recipe uses a home baker's alternative that genuinely works: baking soda, baked for an hour at low heat to drive off moisture and raise its alkalinity. The result is a pretzel that looks and tastes like the real thing — made with einkorn sourdough, shaped by hand, and sprinkled with sea salt. The process is involved but almost entirely passive time. Start in the morning, bake in the afternoon.

Ingredients (makes 10 pretzels)

✦ Making the Sourdough Levain

The levain is a pre-ferment that gives the pretzels their flavour and rise without commercial yeast. Make it 6–10 hours before you plan to mix the dough — the night before works well. New to levain? Read our guide →

Combine: 2 tbsp active einkorn sourdough starter + ½ cup + 1 tbsp warm water (about 125ml) + 120g einkorn all-purpose flour. Mix well, cover, and leave at room temperature until bubbly, doubled, and domed — 6 to 10 hours depending on the warmth of your kitchen. It should smell yeasty and slightly sour, not sharp or alcoholic.

Method

Step 1 — Bake the Baking Soda (1 hour, passive)

  1. Preheat your oven to 275°F (135°C). Spread the baking soda in a single layer in a 9×13 glass or ceramic baking dish.
  2. Bake for 1 hour. This drives off moisture and converts the sodium bicarbonate to sodium carbonate — a stronger alkali that more closely mimics lye's effect on the dough surface. The baking soda will lose about a third of its weight. Remove and set aside to cool. You can do this a day ahead and store in an airtight container.

Step 2 — Mix the Dough

  1. In a medium bowl, combine the levain, warm water, and maple syrup. Stir until the levain is dissolved into the liquid.
  2. In a large bowl, whisk together the einkorn flour and salt. Pour the wet mixture into the dry and mix with a spatula until it comes together into a rough dough.
  3. Add the melted, cooled butter and knead in the bowl with your hands until the dough forms a firm, smooth ball. Einkorn dough will feel slightly denser than standard wheat dough — this is normal. It should be pliable and not sticky.
  4. Cover the bowl with a plate and rest at room temperature for 15 minutes.
  5. Turn the dough once (lift and fold onto itself), then cover lightly with plastic wrap or a damp cloth. Rest at room temperature for 3 hours. The dough will grow but may not double — einkorn rises less dramatically than standard flour.

Step 3 — Shape the Pretzels

  1. Brush a long piece of parchment paper lightly with oil and lay it on your work surface.
  2. Divide the dough into 10 equal pieces. Roll each piece into a tight ball, then rest 5 minutes covered.
  3. Roll each ball into a long rope approximately 22 inches (55cm) long. The centre should be thicker than the ends — the classic pretzel shape requires that the centre bulk up to hold the cross.
  4. Form each rope into a U shape. Lift both ends, cross one over the other twice, then bring the ends down to the bottom of the oval and press gently to adhere, leaving the tips slightly raised.
  5. Place each shaped pretzel on the oiled parchment. Cover loosely with oiled plastic wrap and rest at room temperature for 30–60 minutes. Watch the surface: you want to catch them just before visible bubbles appear on the dough. If the dough over-proofs, the baking soda bath will cause them to collapse.

Step 4 — The Baking Soda Bath & Bake

  1. Preheat your oven to 475°F (245°C). Line two clean baking sheets with fresh parchment.
  2. Prepare two bowls: one with 6 cups of cold clean water; the second with 4 cups of cold water into which you slowly whisk the baked baking soda until dissolved. Handle the baking soda solution carefully — it is alkaline and can irritate sensitive skin.
  3. Working in batches of 2–3 pretzels at a time, lower each pretzel into the baking soda solution. Keep fully submerged for 2 minutes total, turning after 1 minute. The dough will begin to look slightly glossy and leathery.
  4. Lift each pretzel with a wide slotted spoon and dip briefly into the clean water bowl to rinse off excess baking soda. This prevents the surface from tasting soapy.
  5. Arrange the rinsed pretzels on the prepared baking sheets without touching. Sprinkle generously with coarse salt while the surface is still wet so the salt adheres.
  6. Bake at 475°F for 14–16 minutes, until the pretzels are a deep, mahogany brown. This depth of colour is the point — a pale pretzel is an under-baked pretzel. The Maillard reaction needs time and heat.
  7. Cool in the pan for 10 minutes before serving. Pretzels are best eaten the day they are baked, still slightly warm.
✦ Notes & Troubleshooting
  • The baked baking soda is the secret. Do not skip this step and use unbaked baking soda — the result will be pale, bland, and disappointing. One hour in the oven changes its chemistry entirely.
  • Catch the proof at the right moment. Pretzels should go into the baking soda bath before the surface develops visible bubbles. Over-proofed pretzels will collapse and spread. When in doubt, under-proof slightly rather than over.
  • Einkorn specifics: The dough will be stiffer than a standard pretzel dough. Roll slowly and with consistent pressure to get even ropes. If the dough springs back, cover and rest 5 minutes before trying again.
  • The shape: Pretzel shaping takes practice. Your first batch may look rustic — this is perfectly fine and delicious. By the third batch, muscle memory takes over.
  • Variation: For a yeast version, replace the levain with 1 tsp instant yeast dissolved in the warm water, omit the 3-hour rest, and proceed with shaping after a 1-hour rise.
