Starter Care & Maintenance

Keeping the culture alive across the years

"My starter is nine years old. I haven't thrown away any starter since I started it nine years ago. Once it is established, you never have to discard again."

The Refrigerator Method

Once your starter is fully established — reliably active, passing the float test, at least two weeks old — you can store it in the refrigerator between bakes. Cold temperatures slow fermentation to near-standstill, allowing the culture to survive 7–14 days without feeding. This is how most home bakers maintain their starter without daily attention.

⚠ Do Not Refrigerate Before Two Weeks

A starter younger than two weeks has not yet established the stable bacterial and yeast populations it needs to survive cold storage. Refrigerating it too early can kill the cultures. Keep it on the counter, feeding daily or twice daily, until it is reliably active for at least 10–14 consecutive days.

Feeding Ratios

A feeding ratio describes how much fresh flour and water you add relative to the existing starter. Written as starter:water:flour.

RatioEffectWhen to Use
1:1:1 (equal parts)Peaks quickly (3–5 hrs); stronger, more acidic flavourActive baking weeks; warm summer kitchens
1:2:2Peaks in 5–7 hrs; balanced flavourStandard maintenance; most baking situations

The Discard — Nothing is Wasted

During the establishment phase (the first two weeks), you discard starter with each feeding to keep the volume manageable. Once established, you only "discard" when you have more starter than you need — such as when you've been feeding it on the counter and want to reduce volume before the next bake, or when you want to use some in a discard recipe.

Keep a separate discard jar in the refrigerator. Add to it with each feeding. Use it throughout the week in:

  • Crackers (ratio: 4 parts discard to 1 part fat)
  • Pancakes and waffles
  • Pizza dough
  • Sourdough cornbread
  • Hand pie and empanada pastry
  • Flatbread
  • The fried discard snack — direct to a hot pan with olive oil and herbs

Long-Term Storage — The Pioneer Methods

✦ Preserving Without Refrigeration

Short term (days): Shape a stiff ball of starter and bury it in a container of flour. The flour absorbs moisture and slows fermentation significantly. Flatten on top with a little salt crust to seal further.

Medium term (weeks): Bury the starter ball in a canister of salt. The salt draws out moisture, forming a hard crust that preserves the living interior. To revive: chip away the crust, dissolve the inner portion in warm water (discard the salt), leave 2–4 hours, then feed as normal.

Long term (months to years): Spread starter very thinly on parchment paper and leave to dry completely at room temperature — 2–3 days. Crumble into a fine powder. Store in an airtight container away from light and heat. To revive: dissolve in warm water, leave 4–6 hours, then begin feeding once or twice a day. Full activity typically returns within 3–5 days. Pioneer women used this method to carry their starters across oceans.
