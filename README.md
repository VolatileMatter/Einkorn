# The Guardian of the Grain

**Ancient Wisdom, Ancient Bread** — a guide for those who wish to feed their families well, following the forgotten paths of the women who came before.

## About This Project

This is a personal reference site covering einkorn wheat, sourdough bread-making, and the history and mythology woven through the act of baking. It is written for home bakers who want to understand not just the how but the why — the science of fermentation, the nutritional case for ancient grains, and the long tradition of women who kept their families alive through bread.

> **AI Disclosure:** This site was built with heavy use of [Claude AI](https://claude.ai) (Anthropic) — for writing, recipe development, research synthesis, code, and design. All content has been reviewed, shaped, and directed by a human author. Claude was the tool; the intent, voice, and choices are mine.

## Sections

### 📖 Prologue — The Story of Bread

**The History of Bread**
Traces bread from its origins 14,000 years ago through ancient Mesopotamia, Egypt, Rome, the Viking age, medieval Europe, the Slavic tradition, and the industrial revolution. Covers the social and spiritual roles bread has played across civilizations, and what was lost when it became a commodity.

**History of Einkorn**
The story of einkorn wheat specifically — the oldest cultivated grain, its near-disappearance after the Green Revolution, and its rediscovery. Covers what makes it genetically and nutritionally distinct from modern wheat.

### 🌱 The Living Culture — Starter Guides

**Creating Your Sourdough Starter**
A full day-by-day guide to building a sourdough starter from scratch using einkorn flour. Covers equipment, feeding ratios, what to look for at each stage, and troubleshooting a young starter.

**Starter Care & Maintenance**
How to keep your starter alive long-term — refrigerator storage, feeding schedules, how to revive a neglected starter, and how to read your starter's behaviour.

**Levain vs Starter**
Clears up the most common source of confusion in sourdough baking. Explains what a levain is, how it differs from your ongoing starter culture, when to use each, and includes a simple overnight levain formula.

**Converting Yeast Recipes to Sourdough**
A practical conversion guide for any conventional recipe. Covers the one-packet-to-100g equivalence, the flour and liquid adjustment formula, how to use discard as an enrichment in baking-powder recipes, time adjustments, and which recipes are harder to convert.

### 🌿 Working with Einkorn

**Health Benefits of Einkorn**
The nutritional case for einkorn wheat — higher protein, lutein, and mineral content than modern wheat; lower gluten reactivity; significantly lower glycemic response. Covers the science of phytic acid and how long fermentation unlocks minerals.

**Fats & Nutrition**
A guide to the role of traditional fats in nourishing cooking — butter, lard, tallow, olive oil. Covers the science behind fat-soluble vitamins and why these fats were standard in the kitchens this site draws from.

**Working with Einkorn Flour**
Practical technique guide — how einkorn dough behaves differently from modern wheat, hydration adjustments, why it needs less kneading and gentler handling, what to expect at each stage.

**Troubleshooting**
Answers to the most common failures: dense crumb, no rise, gummy interior, flat loaves, over-proofing, under-proofing, and starter problems. Includes a decision-tree format for diagnosing a failed bake.

## Quick Start: Einkorn Sourdough Starter

Everything below assumes you have **einkorn flour** and **filtered water** (or tap water left open for 30 minutes).

**You will need:** a wide-mouth glass jar, a kitchen scale, a rubber band to mark the level.

**Days 1–2 — Begin**
Combine 50g whole grain einkorn flour + 50g warm water in a clean jar. Stir well, scrape down the sides, cover loosely. Leave at room temperature (70–78°F). Do nothing else.

**Days 3–4 — First signs**
Discard all but 50g of the mixture. Add 50g fresh flour + 50g water. Stir, cover, leave. You may see a few bubbles beginning. This is good.

**Days 5–6 — Feeding regularly**
Discard all but 25g. Add 50g flour + 50g water twice a day (morning and evening). Mark the jar with a rubber band after each feeding to track the rise. You are looking for the mixture to rise noticeably between feedings.

**Day 7–10 — Ready to use**
Your starter is ready when it reliably doubles in volume within 4–8 hours of a feeding, smells yeasty and pleasantly sour (not sharp or alcoholic), and passes the float test: a small spoonful dropped in water floats.

Once established, keep your starter in the **refrigerator** and feed it once a week. Pull it out, discard all but a tablespoon, add fresh flour and water, let it peak, then put it back.

## Quick Recipe: Simple Einkorn Sourdough Loaf

**Makes 1 loaf. Takes 2 days (mostly waiting).**

### Ingredients
| | |
|---|---|
| Einkorn all-purpose flour | 420g |
| Whole grain einkorn flour | 180g |
| Warm filtered water | 390g |
| Active sourdough starter | 120g (fed 6–8 hrs before) |
| Fine sea salt | 12g |

### Method

**Day 1 — Morning**
Feed your starter. Wait until it is domed and bubbly (6–8 hours).

**Day 1 — Afternoon**
Mix all the flour and 370g of the water together in a large bowl until no dry patches remain. Cover and rest 45 minutes (autolyse). Dissolve the starter in the remaining 20g water, pour over the dough, then add the salt. Mix gently until fully incorporated.

**Stretch and fold (next 2 hours)**
Every 30 minutes, wet your hands, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over. Turn the bowl a quarter turn. Repeat four times. Do this 3–4 rounds total, then cover and leave undisturbed.

**Bulk ferment**
Leave the dough at room temperature until it has grown 50–75% and looks puffier and more aerated — roughly 4–6 hours depending on kitchen temperature. Einkorn rises less dramatically than modern wheat; watch for feel and bubbles more than size.

**Shape**
Turn the dough out gently. Fold the edges in toward the centre, then flip it seam-side down. Using cupped hands, drag it toward you against the counter to build surface tension. Place seam-up in a well-floured proofing basket (banneton) or a bowl lined with a well-floured cloth.

**Day 1 — Evening**
Cover the basket and put it in the refrigerator overnight. The cold slows fermentation and lets flavour develop over 12–48 hours. The longer it sits, the more complex the loaf.

**Day 2 — Baking**
Place your Dutch oven in the oven and preheat to 500°F (260°C) for at least 45 minutes. When ready, invert the cold dough onto a piece of parchment, score the top with a sharp knife or razor (one confident slash is enough), and lower it into the hot Dutch oven using the parchment as a sling. Put the lid on.

Bake covered 20 minutes. Remove the lid, reduce heat to 450°F, bake 20 more minutes until deep golden brown. Turn the oven off, crack the door, and leave for 15 more minutes. Cool on a rack for at least 1 hour before slicing.
