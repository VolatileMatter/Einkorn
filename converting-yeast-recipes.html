Converting Yeast Recipes to Sourdough

How to bring any conventional recipe into the living tradition

Every recipe that was ever written with a packet of commercial yeast can be made with a sourdough starter instead. The conversion is not complicated — it follows a simple logic. Once you understand it, you will be able to look at any bread, roll, pizza, or batter recipe and know exactly what to do.

Commercial yeast is a single, highly selected strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae, bred to be fast, predictable, and powerful. It does its job in an hour and then it is done. Sourdough starter is a community — wild yeast and Lactobacillus bacteria working together slowly, producing not just carbon dioxide for lift but also lactic and acetic acids that build flavour, lower the glycemic index of the bread, and break down the phytic acid that would otherwise block mineral absorption. The slower process is not a limitation. It is the entire point.

The Core Rule: What to Swap

One standard packet of commercial dry yeast (7g / 2¼ tsp) is equivalent to roughly 100g of active, fed sourdough starter. If a recipe calls for two packets, use 200g of starter. This ratio is a starting point, not a hard rule — a little more starter speeds things up, a little less slows them down. Somewhere between 75g and 150g per standard loaf is the working range for most breads.

Because your starter is itself made of flour and water, it adds both to the recipe. When you add starter, you must reduce the recipe's flour and liquid accordingly — otherwise your dough will be too wet and too loose.

✦ The Conversion Formula (for 100% hydration starter)

A 100% hydration starter is equal parts flour and water by weight — the standard for most home bakers. When you add 100g of this starter to a recipe, you are adding 50g of flour and 50g of water.

  1. Replace yeast: Remove all commercial yeast from the recipe.
  2. Add starter: For every packet of yeast (7g), use 100g of active, fed starter.
  3. Subtract from flour: Reduce the recipe's flour by half the weight of your starter. (100g starter → subtract 50g flour)
  4. Subtract from liquid: Reduce the recipe's water or milk by the other half. (100g starter → subtract 50g liquid)
  5. Adjust if needed: Einkorn absorbs liquid more slowly than modern wheat. Mix, then wait 5 minutes before deciding if the dough needs more flour or liquid.

Quick example: A pizza dough calling for 500g flour, 320g water, and 1 packet yeast becomes: 450g flour + 270g water + 100g active starter. The total weight of the dough stays nearly the same. The flavour and nutrition improve considerably.

Using Discard Instead of Active Starter

Sourdough discard — the portion of starter you remove before feeding — is unfed starter that is no longer at its peak activity. It still contains wild yeast and bacteria, but more dormant ones. Discard is excellent for recipes that have another leavening agent (baking powder or baking soda) doing the heavy lifting, because its job there is flavour and nutrition rather than lift. Pancakes, waffles, muffins, quick breads, scones, flatbreads, crackers, pastry, and dumplings all benefit enormously from discard.

Adding discard to a recipe that already contains baking powder is one of the simplest improvements you can make to any baked good. You are not converting the recipe so much as enriching it — replacing some of the plain flour and liquid with something that carries fermentation benefits. The flavour deepens, the texture often improves, and nothing is wasted.

✦ Adding Discard to Any Existing Recipe

This works for muffins, scones, pancakes, waffles, quick breads, crackers, cookies, and anything with baking powder. The discard is adding flavour and fermentation, not lift.

  1. Decide how much discard to use. A good starting point is 100g (about ½ cup) for a standard batch. You can go up to 200g if you want a more pronounced tang.
  2. Weigh the discard. Divide that weight by two — that is how much flour and how much liquid it contains. (100g discard = 50g flour + 50g liquid)
  3. Reduce the recipe's flour by 50g and its liquid by 50g for every 100g of discard you add.
  4. Add the discard with the wet ingredients.
  5. Proceed as the recipe directs. Rise times are unchanged since baking powder is still doing the leavening work.

Egg shortcut: If a recipe calls for eggs and you are running low, 3 tablespoons of sourdough discard can replace one egg in most batter-style recipes — pancakes, muffins, quick breads. The discard binds and adds moisture in a similar way.

Converting Yeast Breads: Time is the Main Adjustment

Beyond the flour and liquid adjustment above, the other major change when converting a yeast bread is time. Commercial yeast can double a dough in an hour at room temperature. Sourdough starter, working at its natural pace, typically takes 4–8 hours for a bulk fermentation at room temperature, or 12–24 hours in the refrigerator. Both approaches work — the cold method produces more flavour and the bread will hold its shape better.

As a general rule: wherever the original recipe says "let rise until doubled, about 1 hour," replace that with "let rise until 50–75% grown, 3–6 hours at room temperature, or overnight in the refrigerator." Einkorn in particular rises less dramatically than modern wheat — do not wait for a true double. Watch for the dough to look puffier, feel lighter and more aerated when you touch it, and show some bubbles on the surface or sides. That is your cue to proceed.

✦ A Note on Enriched Doughs

Enriched doughs — those with eggs, butter, milk, or sugar — ferment more slowly with sourdough starter than lean doughs do. Fat coats the gluten strands and inhibits fermentation somewhat. Give these doughs more time and a warmer environment, or use a slightly larger quantity of starter (150g rather than 100g). Brioche, cinnamon rolls, enriched dinner rolls, and sweet breads all convert beautifully — they simply ask for patience. An overnight cold proof after shaping is often the easiest approach: shape the dough in the evening, refrigerate overnight, and bake directly from the fridge in the morning.

What About the Starter Itself — Fed or Unfed?

When you are replacing commercial yeast in a bread recipe, your starter must be active and recently fed — at or near its peak, domed and bubbly, with a yeasty smell. Starter that has been sitting in the refrigerator for a week will work, but it will be sluggish and the rise will be unpredictable. For reliable results, feed your starter 6–10 hours before you plan to mix the dough. By the time you are ready to bake, it will be at full strength.

If you forget to feed your starter ahead of time, you have two options: build a quick levain (a spoonful of starter mixed with fresh flour and water, left to ripen for 4–6 hours — see the levain guide), or use a higher quantity of starter and accept a slower, less predictable rise. Both will eventually produce bread. The levain route is more reliable.

Recipes That Are Harder to Convert

Most breads convert without difficulty. A few are more challenging, and worth knowing about before you try.

Pure rye: Rye flour contains very little gluten, which makes it hard to build a dough with structure. A pure rye loaf baked free-form tends to spread rather than hold its shape. If you want to bake with rye, use a loaf tin so the sides support the dough during baking, and expect a denser, more compact crumb — closer to a German-style Vollkornbrot. This is not a failure; it is simply what rye bread is. With einkorn as your primary flour and rye as a portion (up to 20–30%), the conversion works smoothly.

High-enrichment sweet doughs: Recipes with very high quantities of butter, eggs, or sugar (think panettone or brioche) are doable but demanding. The fat slows fermentation significantly and the dough requires multiple builds and close attention. These are rewarding projects but not starting points for a first conversion.

Recipes with very short bake times: Thin cookies, delicate wafers, and quick-cooked items don't need converting — the baking time is too short for fermentation to matter. Use discard simply for flavour in these cases, adding a spoonful to the batter without adjusting anything else. The small amount will not significantly alter the texture, and the flavour will be noticeably more interesting.
