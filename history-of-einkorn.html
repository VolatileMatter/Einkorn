Chapter V

History of Einkorn

The grain that has never been changed

"All the wheat we consume today is descended from einkorn wheat. It is the original grain, in its natural form, the way it was always supposed to be."

Einkorn (Triticum monococcum) is the oldest form of cultivated wheat on earth. It was growing wild when the first human beings decided to stay in one place and tend it. It fed the Neolithic settlements, the Egyptian farmers, the Viking households, the Celtic clans. Unlike all modern wheat, einkorn has never been hybridised. It is not a product of the twentieth century's obsession with yield. It is the original grain, unchanged.

14 Chromosomes vs. 42

Modern wheat contains 42 chromosomes — the result of millennia of intentional and unintentional crossbreeding with wild grasses. Einkorn contains only 14. This is not a trivial difference. Chromosomes carry the genetic instructions for every protein the plant produces, including the proteins that form gluten.

Modern bread wheat (Triticum aestivum) was crossed with two different types of goat grass at some point in agricultural history. These goat grasses contribute what geneticists call the D genome — and it is the D genome that is the primary source of gluten intolerance problems in modern populations. Einkorn contains only the A genome. It does not carry the D genome at all. Most testing for gluten tolerance is based on the D genome. This is why the landscape of sensitivity looks so different for einkorn.

Why Did We Change It?

The honest answer is: yield. Einkorn produces approximately one-fifth the grain per acre of modern dwarf wheat. It grows tall, which means wind can knock it down before harvest. It has a tough husk — a hard coat that must be removed before milling, which requires an additional processing step. None of these things were problems for subsistence farmers growing grain for their own families. All of them became problems when grain became a commodity measured in tons per hectare.

Modern dwarf wheat was bred specifically for the combine harvester — short enough that the machine could cut it without it collapsing first. It was bred for industrial flour mills. It was bred for the factory bakery. None of those breeders were thinking about the health of the people who would eat it. They were thinking about yield.

"There were only a few hundred acres of einkorn being grown worldwide. This ancient grain was on the threshold of extinction." — Carla Bartolucci, founder of Jovial Foods

The Near Extinction of Einkorn

By the mid-twentieth century, einkorn was nearly extinct. It survived in a few isolated pockets — small farms in remote valleys, botanical collections, the margins of agricultural history. The Jovial Foods story is perhaps the most famous einkorn revival narrative: founder Carla Bartolucci, whose daughter was diagnosed with a severe wheat allergy, discovered einkorn through Italian researchers who had been studying it for over a decade. Her daughter's health transformed. The symptoms — asthma, hair loss, abdominal swelling, food sensitivities — all subsided when the family switched to einkorn. When they returned to modern wheat, symptoms returned within hours.

Jovial now farms einkorn organically in Italy and is one of the largest suppliers of einkorn products in the world. Their all-purpose and whole grain einkorn flours are widely available in health food stores and online. They are the gold standard for home bakers.

Einkorn in the Ancient World

Einkorn was one of the two primary crops of the ancient Near East — the other being emmer wheat. Together, these grains fed the Sumerian, Babylonian, and Egyptian civilisations. The Epic of Gilgamesh — the oldest surviving literary text — describes a world built on barley and emmer. When archaeologist Seamus Blackley made bread in 2019 using flour from 4,500-year-old Egyptian artifacts, he was working with einkorn's ancestor. The bread rose. It was edible. The yeast cultures had survived in the grain for millennia.

Ötzi the Iceman — the 5,300-year-old mummy discovered in the Alps in 1991 — had einkorn in his digestive system when he died. Einkorn was his last meal. Of all the forms of wheat that have existed on earth, this is the one our bodies have lived alongside the longest.

Whole Grain vs. All-Purpose Einkorn

All-purpose einkorn flour is sifted to remove about 20% of the wheat berry, resulting in a lighter flour that produces a taller, airier loaf. It is the workhorse for sandwich loaves, rolls, and enriched breads. This is what most of the recipes in this guide use as the primary flour.

Whole grain einkorn flour uses 100% of the wheat berry — bran, germ, and endosperm. Far more nutritious, deeper in flavour, but the bran particles cut gluten strands during mixing, making for a denser loaf. Best used in combination with all-purpose: a 70:30 or 80:20 blend gives you the structure you need without sacrificing the nutrition or flavour that whole grain provides.

On Grinding Your Own

Freshly milled einkorn flour is in a different category from anything sold in a bag. When wheat is milled, its natural oils oxidise immediately on contact with air. Flour that has been in a warehouse for months has already lost its most volatile nutritional compounds. Stone grinding at low temperatures preserves the oils, enzymes, and live compounds that high-speed steel roller mills destroy with heat.

Home grain mills — the Mockmill and NutriMill are popular options — stone-grind einkorn berries at home. Einkorn berries store for years in a cool, dry place. The investment pays for itself in nutrition, flavour, and the quiet satisfaction of milling your own grain as women have done for ten thousand years.
