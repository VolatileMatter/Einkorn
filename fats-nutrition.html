Chapter VII

Fats & Nutrition

How fat transforms bread — and which fat serves your family best

"Any fat will make the bread softer and most will make it stay fresher longer. Flavour and mouthfeel depend on the fat being used."

Fat is not merely an ingredient in bread. It is a transformer. When fat enters the dough, it coats the flour particles and interferes with gluten formation — making the gluten strands shorter and more tender. This is precisely why fat is historically called shortening. Shorter gluten strands mean softer bread, a more tender crumb, and a longer shelf life. Every fat achieves this, but each one does it with a different flavour, texture, and nutritional profile.

How Fat Interacts with Gluten

Flour contains two proteins — glutenin and gliadin — that bond together when water is added to form gluten. These bonds create tiny pockets where CO₂ from fermentation is trapped, which is what makes bread rise. Fat disrupts this by coating the proteins before they fully bond. The result: shorter, more numerous gluten strands rather than long, tough ones. This is desirable for most enriched breads, especially rolls and hand pies, where tenderness matters more than maximum rise.

✦ When to Add Fat

≤10% fat (by flour weight): Add from the beginning. Oils and soft fats blend in easily during initial mixing without inhibiting fermentation.

>10% fat: Develop some gluten through stretch-and-fold first, then add fat in stages. This prevents fat from blocking gluten formation entirely, which would give you a dense, greasy loaf.

Einkorn note: Because einkorn's gluten is already weaker than modern wheat, be cautious with high fat ratios. Enriched einkorn doughs can turn gummy quickly. Build your gluten with folds before adding fat.

The Fats Compared

Grass-Fed Butter

Fat content: 82–85%
Crumb: Super airy, soft, golden-tinged
Flavour: Rich, complex, slightly grassy
Melting point: Body temperature — melts as you eat
Best for: Rolls, enriched loaves, pastry
The most nutritious butter — higher omega-3s, CLA, and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K2) than conventional. The gold standard for enriched bread.

Lard (Home-Rendered)

Fat content: 100%
Crumb: Extremely light and airy
Flavour: Neutral or distinctly porky if from smoked fat
Best for: Pastry, hand pies, rustic loaves
The traditional pastry fat. Home-rendered lard from pasture-raised pigs is rich in fat-soluble vitamins. The most historically authentic fat for pies and bread.

Extra-Virgin Olive Oil

Fat content: 100%
Crumb: Tight, uniform, slightly cake-like
Flavour: Faint to moderate olive note
Melting point: Liquid at room temperature
Best for: Sandwich loaves, flatbread, focaccia
Produces a slightly denser loaf with a beautifully even crumb. As a liquid fat, it slightly increases effective hydration — reduce water by 1 tbsp per 3 tbsp oil used.

Tallow (Grass-Fed)

Fat content: 100%
Crumb: Very tender, light
Flavour: Mild, savoury depth
Best for: Rustic loaves, flatbread, pastry
The fat our ancestors used most. Rich in fat-soluble vitamins when from grass-fed sources. Historically, this was simply called "dripping" and kept on every farmhouse hearth.

Goose or Duck Fat

Fat content: 100%
Crumb: Soft, tender
Flavour: Rich, distinctive, deeply savoury
Best for: Rustic loaves, celebration breads
Render from poultry skins — don't discard them after a roast. Monounsaturated fat profile similar to olive oil but with a roasted, savoury character unlike anything else.

Conventional Butter

Fat content: 80–82%
Crumb: Airy, soft
Flavour: Reliable, mild, familiar
Best for: Any enriched bread recipe
Works well for all enriched recipes. Lower in fat-soluble vitamins than grass-fed, but widely available and still produces excellent results.

For Maximum Nutrition — The Hierarchy

If you are baking with the goal of maximising nutritional density, the fat hierarchy looks like this:

  1. Grass-fed butter or home-rendered lard from pasture-raised pigs. Highest in fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K2) and beneficial fatty acids. The ancestral choice for enriched bread.
  2. Grass-fed tallow or suet. Similar vitamin profile to grass-fed butter. Excellent for pastry and rustic loaves.
  3. Goose or duck fat (home-rendered from pastured birds). Excellent fatty acid profile, rich flavour, underused in modern kitchens.
  4. Extra-virgin olive oil. The best plant-based choice — rich in polyphenols and oleocanthal. Cold-pressed, unfiltered if possible.
  5. Conventional butter or lard. Still good. Use these without guilt when better options are unavailable.

Browning Butter

For quick breads, muffins, and cornbread, browning butter before using it transforms the flavour through the Maillard reaction — the same reaction that browns bread crusts. The milk solids in butter caramelise into golden-brown flecks that taste of toffee and nuts. Brown butter in a light-coloured pan so you can see when the solids turn golden. Pull off heat immediately. Cool before adding to batter. For einkorn specifically, browning butter also slightly reduces its moisture content (the water evaporates during browning), which matters since einkorn already absorbs less liquid than modern wheat.
