Chapter I

The History of Bread

Fourteen thousand years of the oldest alchemy

"Without you, mortals hold no banquet where one does not duly pour sweet wine in offering — Hestia, both first and last."

There is evidence of flatbread dating back 14,000 years. Long before agriculture, before settled civilisations, before writing or law or empire — there was bread. Hunters and gatherers cooked grain paste on smooth hot rocks over open fire. The oldest known bread was not leavened. It was not wheat. It was made from whatever wild grain could be found, ground between stones, mixed with water, and laid on heat. It was simple. It was everything.

Around 10,000 years ago, something shifted. People who had spent millennia following their food began, in various places across the world, to stay still and grow it. The Neolithic Revolution — the transition from foraging to farming — was built on grain. Wheat and barley in the Fertile Crescent. Rice in the Yangtze valley. Maize in Mesoamerica. Millet in sub-Saharan Africa. These grains built the first known cities, created surplus, created trade, created specialisation, and ultimately gave rise to civilisations we recognise as the ancestors of our own.

"Temples weren't just places of worship. They were granaries. People would keep their grain there for the long winters. Priests were considered guardians of the grain and of the harvest." — On the forgotten religion of bread

It was the Egyptians who are credited with the development of the first leavened breads, utilising the wild yeasts and bacteria that fermented the dough, resulting in bread that was softer and airier than its flatbread predecessors. Their technique spread to the Greeks and Romans, who adopted and refined sourdough baking methods. In Rome, bread became so integral to society that the government established a grain dole, providing citizens with free bread to sustain them.

The Medieval Baker

By the Middle Ages in Europe, bread had become so essential to survival that its regulation was a matter of state. In 1266, the English Crown passed the Assize of Bread and Ale — a law that regulated not merely the price but the quality and weight of bread, with brutal punishments for those who cheated. Bakers caught selling underweight loaves were dragged through the streets on a wooden sledge. Because bread was 70% of a peasant's daily calories. Every single day, families ate two to three pounds of dense, dark loaves. When the bread failed, the people failed.

The medieval baker did not bake with commercial yeast — it did not exist. Every morning, he walked next door to the brewery and came home with a bucket of something that looked like toxic sludge: the thick, frothy foam that rose on top of every fermenting barrel of ale. They called it barm. They treated it like liquid gold.

✦ The Secret of Barm

Hidden inside that foam were billions of wild yeast cells and Lactobacillus bacteria — an entire living ecosystem. The yeast ate the sugars and lifted the bread. But the Lactobacillus bacteria were doing something medieval bakers did not even know was happening: they were dismantling phytic acid — a natural toxin in all grains that locks up minerals (iron, zinc, magnesium) and prevents the body from absorbing them.

After 12 to 24 hours of fermentation, the phytic acid was neutralised. The nutrients were unlocked. Medieval peasants were eating pre-digested bread. We are eating a safe with a lost combination. The only difference is time.

The Living Trough

Walk into a medieval bakery and you would see a massive wooden trough — scarred, cracked, stained dark from decades of use. By modern standards, that trough would be condemned. But those cracks were home to a living colony of beneficial bacteria. Every time the baker kneaded dough in that old trough, the wood transferred microorganisms directly into the bread. The trough was more than a tool. It was an ingredient. In some bakeries, the trough was older than the baker himself, passed down from father to son. The wood had memory.

The Grain Mix of Survival

Most medieval peasants ate bread made from maslin — a mix of wheat and rye grown together in the same field. Rye is enzymatically supercharged — it ferments faster and more aggressively than wheat. By mixing rye into the dough, medieval bakers guaranteed a strong, active fermentation. More fermentation meant more phytic acid breakdown. During the Great Famine of 1315 to 1317, summer rains destroyed the wheat harvest. Farmers who had planted only wheat lost everything. Those who had planted maslin survived.

The Death of Real Bread

In 1961, everything changed. Scientists in Chorleywood, England, developed an industrial bread process that replaced 24 hours of fermentation with 3 hours of violent machine mixing, chemical dough conditioners, and extra commercial yeast. The Chorleywood process captured 80% of the UK bread market within a few years. Bakeries could produce thousands of loaves per hour.

But the bacteria never had time to wake up, let alone work. No fermentation meant no acid — so chemical preservatives were added. No fermentation meant no probiotic benefit — so probiotic supplements were later sold separately. Your great-grandmother's bread had gut-protecting cultures built in. We removed the biological system, then sold pharmaceutical replacements.

⚠ The Fake Sourdough Problem

Many supermarket "sourdough" loaves are made using the Chorleywood process with vinegar added to mimic the sour taste. The phytic acid is still fully active. The gluten is unbroken. The bacteria never existed. Real sourdough has three ingredients: flour, water, salt. If the label lists commercial yeast, vinegar, or any multi-syllable chemical additive, it is industrial bread wearing a sourdough label.

The Slavic Sacred Loaf

Among the Slavic peoples of Central and Eastern Europe, bread was not merely food — it was something closer to a divine object. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, oaths were sworn on bread. The Polish word zboże, meaning grain or crops, shares its root with boży, the word for "divine" and "of God." To be without bread was not simply to be hungry; it was to be without the sacred. Bread was baked for every rite of passage — every birth, marriage, death, and festival — and the baking process itself was treated as a spiritual act.

Before fields were planted, ceremonial eggs painted for spring rituals were buried in the first furrow to ensure a good harvest. During the growing season, songs were sung in the fields — not for entertainment but as a form of intercession, because it was believed that song could influence the natural processes of the earth. At harvest, the first sheaf was cut by hand with a sickle, blessed, and saved through winter in the form of the didukh — a sheaf of grain that represented the ancestors and their sacrifice — to be brought out again at the winter solstice. The grain made its way around the entire wheel of the year.

The baking of ritual breads had its own demanding conditions. In some regions, grain for magical bread had to come from a watermill that rotated counterclockwise. In others, the flour had to be passed through an upside-down sieve, or gathered from multiple households, or the baker had to wear new clothes. These conditions served a spiritual purpose: to shift the everyday act of baking slightly out of the ordinary so that the sacred could enter through the cracks. As one Slavic text describes the transformation from grain to loaf: through reaping the wheat "dies" but its seed is born; through milling the seed "dies" but flour is born; flour "dies" but dough is born; dough "dies" but bread is born. Every loaf is a resurrection.

Two ceremonial breads survive into living memory. The kukiełka, still baked in some southern Polish villages, is a soft, sweet braided loaf sometimes reaching four feet in length, woven with intricate patterns and symbols. It is gifted to newborns and given each year until the child turns ten as a talisman of protection. The korowaj — a large round wedding loaf with a top portion representing the moon, decorated with wheat stalks, flowers, stars, and birds — was baked to the sound of women singing traditional songs, their voices woven into the dough as part of the making. Women who specialised in this singing were brought especially to the kitchen. The bread could not properly exist without their voices. Intention was literally baked in.

✦ Slavic Bread as Holy Object

In Slavic folk belief, bread held a power that extended into every domain of life. Some of these beliefs survive in living tradition; others were recorded by ethnographers in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries:

  • In Bosnia, people swore their most solemn oaths on bread — more binding than any legal document.
  • Bread and salt formed the traditional welcome for a guest: to receive this gift was to enter into a bond of hospitality that obligated both parties.
  • A family without bread in the home was considered to be without the divine — a state of spiritual as well as material poverty.
  • Sickly or premature newborns were in some regions "rebaked" in the oven — wrapped in a rye dough and placed briefly in warmth to strengthen them, symbolically completing their time in the womb.
  • The oven was understood as the womb of the mother, and bread as the child born from her body.
  • The movement of the harvest sickle had to follow the path of the sun, from east to west — each cut beginning with the sunrise and ending with the sunset.

The Viking Grain

The Vikings were, first and foremost, farmers. Their bread was made from freshly milled grain — ground by hand on quern stones, mixed sometimes with a splash of beer and coarse salt, and cooked flat on stones by the fire. Archaeological excavations across Scandinavia have found quern stones alongside domestic artifacts in virtually every settlement, indicating that milling was a common household activity — most likely the work of women. The resulting flour was coarser and darker than anything sold commercially today, but freshly milled grain carries its oils, enzymes, and live compounds intact.

The Sourdough Empires

Sourdough built empires in a literal sense. Archaeologists have found evidence of sourdough fermentation in ancient Egypt dating to around 1,500 BC. Ancient Egyptian bakeries produced a wide variety of breads, many using sourdough fermentation, and bread held spiritual and cultural importance beyond simple nutrition. It was the Egyptians who passed this knowledge to the Greeks, who passed it to the Romans, who spread it across Europe through conquest and trade.

During the California Gold Rush, miners known as "sourdoughs" carried their starters with them into the wilderness, and the sourdough tradition in San Francisco flourished — giving rise to the now-famous San Francisco sourdough. Boudin Bakery, established in 1849, is credited with popularising San Francisco sourdough and is still in operation using a starter culture that dates back to the Gold Rush era.

The Pioneer Women

When women crossed the American frontier in the nineteenth century, they carried their sourdough starter with them. They kept their leaven in a pouch of flour around their waists, warmed by their own bodies. At night they set it on warm ground. At midnight they mixed the dough. By morning, bread was ready before the men had rounded up the teams.

"When we camped, I made rising and set on a warm ground. It would be up about midnight. I'd get up and put it to a sponge. In the morning first thing, I would mix the dough and put in the reflector oven. With good hot coals, the breads or cakes for a hearty breakfast were ready by the time the men rounded up the teams." — Pioneer woman's journal, c. 1848

These women had no science. They did not know what Lactobacillus was. But they understood fermentation with their hands and their instincts, and they fed their families well across thousands of miles of wilderness with nothing more than flour, water, fire, and the knowledge passed down from the women before them.

We are attempting to do the same.
