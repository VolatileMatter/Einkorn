Chapter XVII

Troubleshooting

Every failure teaches something. Here is what it teaches.

"Don't be ashamed to screw up or fail. It really is part of the sourdough journey. There are so many variables when you're dealing with natural fermentation — you never really know." — An experienced sourdough baker

Bread Problems

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Dense, heavy loafUnder-fermented or weak starterFeed starter more frequently (twice daily) for 2–3 days. Give bulk fermentation more time.
Impossibly sticky doughOver-mixed, or too warmWet your hands. Chill dough 20 min. Next time stop mixing sooner.
Loaf spreads flat instead of risingDough too wet, weak gluten, or over-proofedUse the fridge proof — cold dough holds its shape. Try slightly less water next time.
Gummy inside after bakingSliced too soon, or under-fermentedWait a full hour before slicing. Extend fermentation next time.
Crust cracks on the sidesScore too deep or near edges, or too much surface tensionScore shallowly (¼ inch) through the centre only. One confident cut.
Bread tastes flat, not sourStarter not at peak; fermentation too shortUse starter at true peak. Extend bulk fermentation. Consider longer fridge proof.
No oven springOver-proofed; gluten exhaustedUse poke test. Bake earlier next time. Poke should spring back slowly.
Bottom crust burnsDutch oven too hot at basePlace a second baking tray underneath the Dutch oven to insulate the base.
Ale bread too bitterHeavily hopped IPA usedSwitch to an amber ale, stout, or dark beer. Avoid hop-heavy beers.
Rolls came out flatFat added before gluten developedAdd fat only after the first folds have built some structure. Work it in gently.

Starter Problems

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Not bubbling after 10 daysCold kitchen, chlorinated water, weak local yeast populationMove to warmer location. Use bottled water. Try one rye feeding. Add date-water (sieved through dates).
Black liquid on topHooch — over-fermented, hungryPour off. Feed twice daily until active again.
Acetone smellVery hungry, high acid productionFeed more frequently until smell shifts to pleasantly sour/fruity.
Not rising at peak anymoreWeakened culture; needs strengtheningFeed twice daily for 3–5 days. Consider adding whole grain flour to feedings.
Pink or orange streaksDangerous contaminationDiscard entirely. Start over. This is the only situation requiring this action.
Starter smells too sour / vinegaryProduces more acetic than lactic acid; too coldFeed more frequently. Keep warmer (above 72°F).

Pastry & Cracker Problems

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Pastry cracks when foldingToo cold, or too much flourLet pastry rest at room temperature 5 minutes before rolling.
Hand pies burst in ovenOverfilled, or vents missingLess filling. Cut two clear slits before baking. Seal edges more firmly.
Crackers go softStored warm or in humid environmentEnsure fully cooled before sealing. Keep in a dry, airtight container.
Crackers burnSpread too thin at edgesAim for even thickness. The edges always bake faster — watch them.

A Note on Patience

Sourdough baking has more variables than almost any other cooking. The strength of your starter, the temperature of your kitchen, the humidity of the air, the protein content of your flour, the specific bacteria living in your culture — all of these affect the outcome. A recipe is a map, not a road.

The medieval baker had no recipe. She had years of practice — hands that could feel the difference between a dough that was ready and one that needed more time, a nose that knew when the fermentation had gone far enough. You are building that same knowledge. Every loaf — good, mediocre, or failed — teaches something.
