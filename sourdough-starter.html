Chapter IX

Creating Your Sourdough Starter

Capturing the wild yeasts of your own kitchen

"The sourdough starter is the original way bread was made for thousands of years. It is a culture for the wild yeast and bacteria in your own environment — of your place."

A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and Lactobacillus bacteria, captured from your flour and the air of your kitchen. It is not interchangeable with anyone else's starter. It will reflect the wild yeasts in your home, your flour, your hands — and it grows more stable and more potent with every year you tend it.

Once established, a starter can last for the rest of your life and beyond. There are starters in use today that are more than a century old. The ancient Mesopotamian bakers who made the first leavened bread were tending cultures not functionally different from what you are about to make. The process has not changed. The knowledge is the same.

✦ Before You Begin — Equipment
  • Glass or ceramic jar — metal reacts with the acids the culture produces. A wide-mouth quart jar is ideal.
  • Filtered or bottled water — chlorine in tap water inhibits the cultures. If you only have tap water, let it sit open for 30 minutes first.
  • Whole grain einkorn flour to start — the bran carries more wild yeast than white flour.
  • A warm location — 70–80°F. Near the stove, in an oven with just the light on, or wrapped in a cloth near a fire.
  • A rubber band or marker — to track the height of your starter before and after feeding.

The Ancient Mesopotamian Method

The Sumerians and Egyptians made their starters using 50% emmer wheat and 50% barley flour — grains high in wild yeast and enzymes. You can make an historically authentic starter by using a blend of any two ancient grains: einkorn and rye, einkorn and barley, or even pure einkorn. Some bakers follow the ancient method of running water through a sieve containing dates — the skin of dates carries wild yeast, just as grape skins do — and using this enriched water to start the culture. It genuinely works.

Day-by-Day Guide

Select a day and follow through. The process takes 7–10 days. Do not rush it. Temperature is the largest variable — a cool kitchen extends the timeline, a warm one shortens it.

Day 1 — The Beginning

  1. In a clean glass jar, combine 60g whole grain einkorn flour and 45g filtered water (about 75°F). Stir thoroughly until no dry flour remains. The mixture will be a thick, sticky paste.
  2. Loosely cover — not airtight, as fermentation produces gas. A cloth secured with a rubber band works well.
  3. Leave at room temperature (70–80°F) for 24 hours. Mark the level on the outside of the jar.

Do not expect activity on Day 1. You are creating conditions, not observing results yet. The wild yeasts are just beginning to wake.

Day 2 — The First Signs

You may see a few bubbles — or nothing at all. Both are completely normal. Stir the mixture. It may already smell slightly sour or earthy.

If you see a grey or brown liquid on top, this is hooch — alcohol produced by fermentation. Pour it off and stir. It means the starter is already producing something, and is hungry.

The smell may be strange — not appetising — at this stage. Competing bacteria are present and will produce off-odours. They will be outcompeted by the good cultures within a few days. This is normal.

Day 3 — First Feeding

The starter may show a grey surface. Scrape it aside and use the creamy, active portion beneath.

  1. Keep 10g of starter. Discard the rest (or use in crackers, flatbread, or any discard recipe).
  2. Add 30g of filtered water. Dissolve the starter in the water — stir until the water looks slightly milky.
  3. Add 60g of whole grain einkorn flour. Mix thoroughly. No dry patches.
  4. Transfer to a clean jar. Cover loosely. Mark the level. Rest 24 hours.

Days 4–5 — Building Momentum

Repeat the Day 3 feeding each day: discard to 10g, dissolve in 30g water, add 60g flour. You should begin to see more consistent bubbling.

The smell should be shifting from strange or funky toward something more pleasant — sour and fruity, like overripe fruit, mild beer, or yoghurt. This is good.

✦ The Rye Trick

If things seem slow, substitute rye flour for one feeding instead of einkorn. Rye is enzymatically supercharged and carries more wild yeast than almost any other grain. A single rye feeding will often kick-start a sluggish culture dramatically. You can return to einkorn for subsequent feedings.

Days 6–7 — Strengthening

Begin feeding twice a day — once in the morning and once at night. Same ratio: 10g starter, 30g water, 60g flour. Between feedings, watch for the starter to rise, dome, and then begin to fall. The point just before it falls — called the peak — is when it is most active and most suitable for baking.

You may now begin switching to all-purpose einkorn flour for a milder flavour, or continue with whole grain for a more robust culture. Many bakers keep their starter on whole grain flour permanently for the stronger yeast population.

Days 8–10 — Final Establishment

Continue twice-daily feedings. By this point the starter should:

  • Reliably double in size within 4–8 hours of feeding
  • Show a clear dome of bubbles at its peak
  • Smell consistently sour, fruity, and alive — something between beer and vinegar and fresh bread
  • Pass the float test (see below)

Is It Ready?

Your starter is ready when it reliably doubles in volume within 4–8 hours of a feeding and the surface shows a dome of bubbles at its peak. Use the float test to confirm:

  1. Fill a glass with cool water.
  2. Drop a small spoonful of starter (about ½ teaspoon) into the water.
  3. If it floats on the surface, the culture is producing enough gas to leaven bread. It is ready.
  4. If it sinks, return it to its jar and continue feeding. Test again in 12–24 hours.
⚠ The One True Emergency

If you see pink or orange streaks anywhere in the starter at any point, discard the entire batch and begin again from scratch. This indicates harmful contamination — Serratia marcescens or similar pathogens — and cannot be resolved by feeding. This is the only situation that requires starting over. All other strange signs — grey surface, black liquid (hooch), unusual smells — are normal stages that resolve with continued feeding.

Your Starter Has a Name

Many bakers name their starters. This sounds eccentric until you consider that you are tending a living community that will be with you for years, that reflects your home and your hands, and that will nourish everyone you love. Naming it is the natural response to recognising a relationship.
