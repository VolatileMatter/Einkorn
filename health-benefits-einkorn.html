Chapter VI

Health Benefits of Einkorn

What ancient grain, slow fermentation, and living culture do for the body

"Making bread with a sourdough culture and einkorn flour is a win-win when it comes to deactivating phytic acid. One sandwich can give you 30% of the magnesium you need in a day."

The health case for einkorn sourdough is not merely a matter of tradition or sentiment. It is biological, measurable, and backed by both ancient practice and modern nutritional research. Understanding why this bread is different — structurally, chemically, and nutritionally — helps you make it with greater intention and share it with greater confidence.

The Phytic Acid Problem

All grains contain phytic acid, also called phytate. Phytic acid is a compound the grain uses to store phosphorus for the seedling's use when it germinates. The problem for humans is that phytic acid is a powerful mineral binder — it locks onto iron, zinc, calcium, and magnesium in the gut and carries them out of the body before they can be absorbed. This is called an anti-nutrient effect. Bread made with modern fast-fermentation processes (including most commercial sourdoughs) contains fully active phytic acid. The minerals are present but locked away.

Einkorn has approximately 50% less phytic acid than modern wheat to begin with. And sourdough fermentation — specifically the Lactobacillus bacteria in a healthy starter — produces phytase enzymes that neutralise phytic acid progressively over the course of a long fermentation. After 12–24 hours, the anti-nutrient effect is significantly diminished. The minerals are freed. Your body can absorb them.

✦ The Fermentation Window for Phytic Acid

Phytic acid begins breaking down within the first 2 hours of fermentation, but significant reduction requires a minimum of 7–8 hours. An overnight cold proof (12–14 hours total fermentation time) is the gold standard for maximum phytic acid deactivation. This is why all slow-fermented loaf recipes in this guide include an overnight fridge proof. It is not merely for flavour — it is nutritional alchemy.

The Gluten Difference

Einkorn does contain gluten — it is not gluten-free. However, its gluten is structurally different from modern wheat in ways that matter enormously for digestion.

Modern bread flour is considered good quality for baking when it has a high content of high-molecular-weight glutenin proteins, which create the strong, elastic, extensible gluten network that industrial baking requires. Einkorn is lacking in certain high-molecular-weight glutenin proteins. This is why einkorn dough is stickier and less elastic than modern wheat dough — it handles differently. But this is precisely what makes it more digestible.

More specifically, researchers have found that an entire group of w-gliadins present in other wheat are completely absent in einkorn. These proteins are among the primary triggers for immune responses in gluten-sensitive individuals. Einkorn also has a significantly different ratio of gliadin to glutenin proteins compared to modern wheat. The gluten it does contain behaves differently in the body — and for many people with sensitivity to modern wheat, this difference is life-changing.

⚠ Important: Einkorn Is Not Coeliac-Safe

Einkorn contains gluten and is not safe for people with diagnosed coeliac disease. The gluten is structurally different and more easily digested by many, but it is still present. If you have coeliac disease, do not eat einkorn without medical guidance. If you have non-coeliac gluten sensitivity, many people find einkorn well-tolerated — but individual responses vary.

Gut Health and the Living Starter

Beyond the grain itself, the sourdough fermentation process produces a bread that is genuinely probiotic and prebiotic in its effect. The Lactobacillus bacteria in a mature starter are the same genus as the beneficial bacteria found in healthy human guts. During fermentation, they:

  • Break down phytic acid, freeing minerals for absorption
  • Partially pre-digest the gluten proteins, reducing the immune burden
  • Produce lactic and acetic acid, which lower the bread's pH, acting as natural preservatives and improving gut microbiome composition
  • Produce B vitamins including folate as byproducts of their fermentation
  • Break down some of the fermentable oligosaccharides that cause bloating in many people

Many people who report difficulty with modern bread — bloating, sluggishness, brain fog, skin reactions — find that slow-fermented einkorn sourdough produces none of these effects. This is not anecdotal mysticism. It is the difference between a bread whose proteins are largely intact and undigested, and a bread whose proteins have been partially disassembled by 18 hours of bacterial activity.

The Gut Healing Note

If your gut is significantly compromised — if you have a history of digestive illness, food sensitivities, or have been eating processed food for many years — the bacteria that digest grain may be genuinely missing or depleted. This is not a permanent state, but it means the transition to traditional foods requires patience. Start with small amounts. Include other fermented foods: yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut. Give your gut microbiome time to rebuild. The bread will become easier as the bacteria return.

The Glucose and Fat Metabolism Connection

Research has found that einkorn decreases cell production related to glucose and fat metabolism in ways that modern wheat does not — potentially helping prevent or delay the development of metabolic disease. The combination of lower glycaemic index, higher protein content, and greater mineral availability makes it a bread that genuinely nourishes rather than merely filling a caloric hole.

One sandwich of einkorn sourdough — two slices of well-fermented bread — can provide approximately 30% of your daily magnesium requirement, significant zinc and B6, substantial protein, and a meaningful reduction in the inflammatory load compared to industrial bread. It is not a superfood in the marketing sense. It is simply real food, slowly made, from an ancient grain that our bodies recognise.
