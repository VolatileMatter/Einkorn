Chapter XXV

Diana

Huntress, moon goddess, Queen of Witches — she who knows the wild and keeps it

"Diana of the crossroads, Diana of the hunt, Diana who gives and takes the light — stand at the threshold between the wild and the home."

There is a goddess who moves through the forest at night with a silver bow, and the animals do not run from her. They come closer. She is not the domesticator — she is the wild force itself, wearing a human face, and the deer and the hound and the black cat who sleeps in the kitchen all answer to her. Diana, Roman goddess of the hunt and the moon, is perhaps the most misunderstood deity in the classical world — reduced in the popular imagination to a simple huntress, a female counterpart to Apollo, a virginal figure with a bow. She is all of those things and something much stranger: she is the keeper of the boundary between the tamed and the untamed, the goddess of every threshold, the one who understands that civilisation is built on wildness and cannot be severed from it without catastrophe.

Who Is Diana?

Diana is the Roman goddess of the hunt, the moon, childbirth, nature, and the protection of women. Her Greek counterpart is Artemis, though Diana is in several respects distinct — she has deeper roots in the Italian peninsula and in older strands of folk religion that predate the Greek influence on Roman culture. Her cult centre at Lake Nemi, in the Alban Hills south of Rome, was considered one of the most sacred sites in Italy — a woodland sanctuary where she was worshipped under the title Diana Nemorensis, Diana of the Grove, by women seeking her aid in childbirth and by those who lived outside the boundaries of Roman social order.

She was called the Queen of Witches — Regina Strigum — not as an insult but as a recognition. The witches of folk tradition, the streghe of Italian popular belief, understood themselves as Diana's followers. She presided over the night, the moon, the crossroads, the wild places beyond the edge of the city. These were the domains of magic — of forces that operated outside the ordered, masculine, civic structures of Roman life. Diana held all of that. She was sovereign over the unseen.

The August Full Moon

In ancient Rome, the Full Moon of August was sacred to Diana. This was the month of the harvest — the time when grain was being cut and stored, when the year's labour was being gathered in, when the threshold between abundance and scarcity was at its most visible. Diana's festival, the Nemoralia, fell on the thirteenth of August. Women walked in procession to her sacred grove at Lake Nemi carrying torches, their hair crowned with garlands, making offerings in exchange for prayers granted over the previous year.

The timing is significant. August is the month when Virgo rises — the sign of the harvest, the grain, the embodied knowledge of nourishment. Ceres is its patron. But Diana, arriving in the August Full Moon's light, brings the other half of the picture: she is the wildness that makes the harvest possible. The deer that was hunted. The forest that provided shelter and fuel for the fire. The untamed world that existed before any field was planted and that will continue after the last one is abandoned. You cannot have the harvest without the wild. You cannot have Ceres without Diana.

The Hunt and the Harvest — Two Faces of Nourishment

In the ancient world, the harvest festival and the hunting season were not separate events in the calendar of nourishment — they were its two chambers, beating together. The grain was cut in August. The hunt began in August. Both were occasions for ceremony, for gratitude, for acknowledgement of the life that was being taken so that other lives could continue. The difference between hunter and farmer in this older understanding is not the difference between violence and peace — it is the difference between two modes of the same fundamental act: taking life from the living world and transforming it into nourishment.

Among Indigenous peoples of North America, this understanding was — and remains — explicit. Before a hunt, there was ceremony: a visioning, an honouring, a communication of gratitude to the herd from which a life would be taken. The animal who was killed was understood to have given itself — a voluntary sacrifice, a reciprocal exchange within a web of relationships. The hunter who made that kill was obligated to honor it in ceremony as well. Nothing casual. Nothing taken for granted. Nothing wasted. Diana, in her Roman form, carried this same ethic: she was the huntress, yes, but she was also the protector of wild animals, the guardian of the herd. She gave golden arrows to the creatures under her care. She both presided over the hunt and protected the hunted. She held both truths simultaneously.

✦ Diana as Guardian of Wild Creatures

Diana is often depicted with a deer — her sacred animal — and is associated with all wild creatures and the natural world they inhabit. In Roman religion, she was simultaneously the goddess who enabled the hunt and the goddess who protected animals from unnecessary harm. This apparent paradox resolves when you understand that the hunt, done rightly, is a relationship of respect rather than domination.

Her other sacred animal is the black cat — a creature that stands at the threshold between domestic and wild, between the familiar and the uncanny. It sleeps by the fire but will not be fully tamed. It hunts by night. It sees in the dark. The black cat belongs to Diana precisely because it belongs nowhere completely — it inhabits the boundary, just as she does.

Diana, Spells, and Sacred Speech

Diana's connection to magic is ancient and deep. The word that describes her work — the word spell — carries its own history worth recovering. It comes from the Germanic spel, which means talk, story, gossip, sermon. Spel is also a root of gospel — the good tale. A spell, at its root, is a story told with intention. It is speech that shapes reality. Prayer is a spell. Ritual is a spell. The songs the Slavic women sang in the fields to encourage the grain to grow were spells. The words spoken over bread dough before it is shaped — spoken or unspoken, conscious or habitual — are spells.

Diana presides over this kind of working. Not the elaborate ceremonial magic of grimoires and alchemical symbols, but the older and more practical magic of tending — the knowing attention given to living things to help them thrive. The knowledge of wild medicines and cycles that is described in the oldest accounts of Diana is exactly this: an embodied understanding of how things grow and what they need, which plant is healing and which is harmful, when to act and when to wait. This is not separate from what we do when we bake bread. It is the same knowledge. The woman who manages a sourdough starter is working with a living organism that responds to temperature and timing and attention. She is doing, in a domestic key, what Diana does in the wild.

Diana and Femininity

Diana is famously sovereign unto herself. She asked her father Jupiter for eternal chastity — not as a constraint but as a freedom, a refusal to be defined by her relationship to any man. She is complete within herself. She is, in this sense, the archetype of the woman who draws her identity not from her role as wife or mother but from her own nature, her own powers, her own domain. The forest is hers. The moonlight is hers. The creatures of the wild answer to her and she to them.

But she is not cold. Diana is fiercely protective of those under her care — particularly women in vulnerability. She was the patron of childbirth (alongside Lucina), invoked by women in labour. She gave special protection to slaves and to those outside the structures of power — her sanctuary at Nemi was a place where the marginalised could find refuge. The huntress who lives beyond convention has particular sympathy for those whom convention has failed.

She reminds us that femininity is not only the domestic and the nourishing — it is also the wild, the autonomous, the sovereign, and the free. Ceres feeds. Diana hunts. Both are necessary. Both are feminine. The home and the forest are not opposites; they are in relationship, each sustaining the other, each requiring the other to exist. The bread on the table and the deer in the grove are part of the same story.

Diana and the Sourdough Kitchen

It may seem a stretch to bring Diana into the bread-baking kitchen. She is a goddess of forests and moons and wild things, and the kitchen is the most domestic of spaces. But consider: the sourdough starter is a wild thing. It was captured from the air — wild yeasts and bacteria that exist in the environment, drawn down into flour and water and coaxed into a living culture. Every time you feed your starter, you are maintaining a relationship with wildness. You have domesticated it, somewhat, but you have not tamed it. It still follows its own rhythms. It responds to seasons and temperatures that no recipe can fully account for. It is, in a genuine sense, alive and wild — and it lives in your kitchen.

Diana governs that threshold. She is the force that makes it possible to stand at the edge of the wild and bring something useful back from it without destroying it in the taking. The sourdough baker who cultivates a multi-year starter, who watches and learns the particular personality of her culture, who works with its wildness rather than against it — she is doing something Dianaic. She is the huntress who knows the forest well enough to move through it without breaking it.

✦ The Lunar Kitchen

Baking by the lunar calendar has a long history in folk tradition. The waxing moon, associated with growth and rising, was considered favorable for bread-making — the same principle that governs planting by the moon. The full moon, Diana's most powerful phase, was a time of maximum energy and maximum light.

Many practitioners of folk magic and earth-based spirituality continue to align their baking with the lunar cycle. Whether or not you hold any belief in the mechanism, there is something grounding about orienting your domestic practice to the sky. Diana invites us to remember that the kitchen is not separate from the natural world — it is the place where the natural world is transformed into nourishment, and that transformation is always a little bit magical.

An Invocation for the Wild and the Tended

Diana — who moves through the dark without fear,
who knows the difference between what is yours to take
and what must be left living —

stand at the threshold of this kitchen.
Let what is wild in this dough stay wild.
Let it rise on its own time, its own terms.
Let me be the kind of baker who listens
before she acts.

And let the bread that comes from this wildness
feed whoever needs feeding.
