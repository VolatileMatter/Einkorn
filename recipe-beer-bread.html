Ancient Ale Bread
The medieval baker's secret — ale and overnight slow fermentation

The medieval baker collected barm every morning from the brewery next door — the living foam skimmed from fermenting barrels of ale. This bread honours that tradition. The ale adds residual wild yeast, natural sugars that caramelise into a deeply coloured crust, and a flavour complexity that cannot be replicated any other way.

✦ Choosing Your Ale

Use a real, unfiltered ale if possible — the live yeast cells contribute directly to fermentation. A dark amber ale or stout adds molasses-like depth and a rich mahogany colour. A lighter ale gives a more delicate result with a faint hop note. Avoid heavily hopped IPAs, which can make the bread bitter.

The ale must be at room temperature before use — cold ale shocks the starter and slows fermentation. If you brew at home, skimming barm directly from an active fermentation and using it in place of (or alongside) the starter is the most historically authentic approach possible.

Research confirms that beer was the standout in alternative liquid experiments for bread — it adds flavour, residual yeast, and the sugars cause deeper browning than plain water. Use the beer you love to drink.

240g (1 cup) — room temperature
Filtered water (warm)
120g

100g — fed and bubbly
Fine sea salt
11g (2 tsp)
Butter or lard (optional)
20g softened — enriches crumb slightly

Method

Day 1 — Mix & Ferment

  1. Combine both einkorn flours in a large glass bowl.
  2. Whisk room-temperature ale and warm water together. Add the active starter and stir to dissolve.
  3. Pour the ale mixture over the flour. Mix until no dry patches remain. Cover and autolyse 45 minutes.
  4. Add salt (and fat if using). Fold gently until incorporated. Cover. Rest 30 minutes.
  5. Perform 3 rounds of stretch-and-fold, 20–30 minutes apart.
  6. Bulk ferment at room temperature 4–6 hours until puffy and bubbly, OR refrigerate overnight for a longer, more flavourful fermentation — strongly recommended for maximum phytic acid breakdown.

Day 2 — Shape & Bake

  1. Shape the cold dough gently. Place seam-up in a well-floured proofing basket. Refrigerate 1–2 hours, or proof at room temperature 45–60 minutes. Cold dough is much easier to score.
  2. Preheat Dutch oven at 500°F for 45–60 minutes.
  3. Invert dough onto parchment. Score: two parallel slashes, or a simple cross — medieval in character.
  4. Bake covered 30 minutes. Remove lid. Bake 15–20 more minutes at 450°F until deep mahogany brown. The ale sugars caramelise darker than plain sourdough — this is correct.
  5. Optional curing: oven off, door cracked, 20 minutes. Cool 1 hour before slicing.
✦ Why Overnight Fermentation Matters Here

The ale introduces additional sugars and wild yeast that drive a more aggressive fermentation. Over 12–18 cold hours, the Lactobacillus bacteria have full time to work through the phytic acid in both flour varieties. The result is not only more complex in flavour but genuinely more nutritious — the minerals in your einkorn are fully unlocked and bioavailable.
